PROJECT MAMMOTH

Project Mammoth is an interactive hologram installation designed by Brock University's Project Mammoth student team. The installation is based on biodiversity loss and the impact of human activies on marine species, aiming to providing a unique educational experience for all ages. Combining hologram technology with audio/visual and motion components, it encourages educators to consider new methods of presenting critical topics that would keep viewers more engaged than a traditional lecture or seminar.

This installation is the first project of its kind to be created in Brock University's Interactive Arts & Sciences program. It was put together by a team of twelve third-year students over a span of eight months and displayed in the university's Digital Scholarship Lab. I was appointed project lead for the installation and was part of the modelling team for asset creation.

CONCEPT ORIGINS

The idea to create a hologram installation as part of an interactive project stemmed from videos of DIY holograms using a phone and part of a plastic CD case. I never had the opportunity to put one together myself, however I thought it would be interesting to see whether this makeshift version could be scaled up for an educative purpose. Along with the members of Project Mammoth, we worked together to brainstorm different topics that would benefit from a visual approach; unanimously, it was ageed upon that biodiversity loss was something we all felt passionate about to create a project for.

Our research took us into a different direction than was originally expected with such a topic. Instead of focusing solely on endangered marine species that were well-known, we selected additional animals that were less spoken about by the media and environmental groups, such as the blue-footed booby and the acropora jacquelinae coral. We felt it was important to note that biodiversity loss was more serious than most people are aware of, hence our decision to dig deeper. By the end of our research period, the team had selected a total of eight unique animals.

MODELLING & ASSET CREATION

One of the key components of the installation was creating 3D models of each animals that we'd chosen to showcase. The modelling team, which consisted of myself and three other members, selected a low-poly modelling style that would appear friendly and playful, while simultaneously allowing us to add physical details and markings that were unique to each animal. Each model was created and textured in Blender, then rendered for the project's website and design document in a high quality format. I created four of the eight animal models: the African penguin, the striped smooth hound shark, the acropora jacquelinae coral, and the Arctic polar bear.

For the visual component of the hologram, the modelling team experimented with several variations of environment styles that would work best with the plexiglass prism we intended to create for the reflections. We felt it would be more beneficial in communicating our message of awareness if habitats were included, as biodiversity loss often begins with the destruction of such areas. It was decided that the environments would mimic small domes or "terrariums", and would open to reveal the associated animal. Each member of the modelling team created the environment for their respective animals, then passed it along to me for rendering in the video format that would be used for the hologram.

INTERACTIVE COMPONENTS & SOUND

Interactivity was a part of the project that we had to refine through several iterations. It was already known that the hologram would require sound of some sort, which we achieved through educational narration about each animal and custom audio tracks mixed together by members of the sound team. However, one of our biggest issues stemmed from the question of how to combine the visuals and audio with our planned interactive component ‚Äî a motion sensor that would allow viewers to switch to the next animals using gestures. After several troubleshooting sessions and endless debugging, the coding team was able to create a Python program that communicated with a Raspberry Pi and Flick motion sensor board. The program would pull from an array of videos that we created to work according to the reflective physics of the plexiglass prism; all narration and music would already be integrated into the videos to lighten the load on the Python program.

The Flick motion sensor was operated using four simple air-based gestures: swipe to the right to go to the next animal; swipe to the left to go to the previous animal; swipe upwards to increase the volume; swipe down to decrease the volume. We decided to stick to these gestures in order to avoid issues with the motion sensor deciphering movements incorrectly, as well as to allow younger viewers to be able to operate the hologram as easily as possible.

PROJECT BRANDING

Project Mammoth's branding was created by members of the branding team, and was designed to encapsulate a playful approach to the installation. We envisioned our project as something that would cater to audiences of all ages, so we wanted our branding to share the message that education was for everyone. Upon initial feedback and review of our project concept, the question was raised of why a mammoth was chosen as part of the logo and project name, despite it not being a marine animal. Mammoths are well-known for being a species that went extinct shortly after the Earth transitioned out of the Ice Age, however some scientists believe the mammal was lost due to additional pressure of human hunting. The animals we chose for the project are facing endangerment for similar reasons as the mammoth ‚Äî habitat loss through global warming and human involvement. It serves as a warning that if humanity continues to disregard its impact on nature, the animals we share the planet with will face the same fate as mammoths did all those years ago.
