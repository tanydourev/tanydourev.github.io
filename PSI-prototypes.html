PSI Prototypes*

Creating adoption opportunities for senior animals through design.

Course: Prototyping Services & Interfaces
Year: 2024
Semester: 2

As part of the final project for my Masters studies in UX and service design, I explored the topic of senior animal adoption through a human-centred perspective, and conducted academic/user research to better understand the factors that affect the adoptability of senior cats and dogs. A series of three prototypes were developed, depicting preliminary design concepts that will be used to guide the project's development moving forward.

THE RESEARCH PROBLEM

How can we use design to create adoption opportunities for senior animals and support pet owners by providing better access to caretaking resources?

Senior cats and dogs have long been subjected to reputations as loving companions who are the last to leave shelters, with adoptability rates being significantly lower than their younger counterparts (Afonso et al., 2024; Hawes et al., 2018; Lampe et al., 2015; Markowitz, 2019). Although these animals are documented to provide significant health benefits for humans (Putney, 2012; Sable, 2013), many adopters are still hesitant to take on the challenge of caring for an older pet due to factors such as existing medical conditions, shorter lifespans, and prior sociability with other pets/children (Hawes et al., 2018). As a result, extended stays in shelters demoralize adopting prospects and negatively impact the health of senior animals (Dybdall et al., 2014; Workman et al., 2015; Hawes et al., 2018).



KEY INSIGHTS

Senior animals fight for adopter attention.

In 2021, Humane Canada recorded an intake of over 81,000 animals (2021) ‚Äî with such high levels of competition for adopter attention, it comes to no surprise that senior animals struggle to be noticed amidst such numbers. For many adopters, having this variety of choices can be a challenge for senior animals, who are already disadvantaged due to their age.

Unlike younger animals, there are more factors affecting the adoptability of senior animals:

üéÇ Age

Senior animals have shorter lifespans, which can deter an adopter who is looking for a long-term pet.

üíä Medical Conditions

As senior animals age, they are at greater risk of developing medical conditions that may require medications or treatments.

üê± Sociability

Not all senior animals do well around other pets or children, which can deter adopters who have families or other animals.

üêæ Special Needs

Some senior animals have special needs, require a specific caretaking regime or may suffer from disabilities.

‚ú® Appearance

Specific coat or fur colours are viewed as less desirable by adopters, such as animals with darker/black colours.

üêï Temperament

Senior animals can be less friendly as they age, a factor that is more prevalent in those who have stayed in shelters for longer.



THE SOLUTION

Creating a senior animal adoption service.

Many commercial adoption services promote senior animals on their platforms, but there is no dedicated adoption service that solely targets this age demographic. This provides a unique opportunity to create a space where greater attention can be placed on senior animals, offering support for their needs and encouraging adopters to consider older companions while simultaneously aiding shelters in improving the intake-to-adoption conversion. The service will also be supported by user research derived from pet owners, adopters, and animal shelters, emphasizing the importance of addressing user needs.

Through a needs analysis, the following user needs and pain points were identified, which guided the development of the prototypes:

User Needs

  • Ease of adoption/location
    Where can people adopt senior animals? Which shelters house senior animals in need of a home?
  • Access to information
    How do you take care of a senior animal? What kinds of medical issues should an adopter look out for?

Pain Points

  • Emotional attachment: Senior animals have shorter lifespans; owners aren‚Äôt prepared to deal with the death of a pet; the needs of senior animals can be intimidating for first-time adopters.
  • Support for caretaking: Adopters require easier access to information on senior animals; the variety of medical issues associated with senior animals can be confusing.



PROTOTYPE #1

Service user flow.

The user flow is designed to be a mid-level overview of how a user would interact with the homepage of the senior animal adoption service. It highlights the primary functions and interactions with additional details, but represents only a small portion of the full interactions available. The intention for the service is to provide a space where senior animals can be viewed and adopted, and to create a hub for pet owners looking to find information on adopting senior animals. Many adopters also feel that they are not equipped with the proper knowledge to care for a senior animal; having a reputable and trustworthy source of caretaking information can help ease the worries of adopters and encourage them to make informed adoption decisions.



PROTOTYPE #2

Storyboard scenario.

The storyboard scenario provides a glimpse into how a user would interact with the service as a whole ‚Äì this includes the desktop website, the mobile app, and associated social media networks. I felt that including the different platforms on which the service would be available or would display information on better represented ways in which a user would access it.

Through Miro, I brainstormed the different aspects of the storyboard that I wanted to focus on, such as the issues/pain points, user motivations, and feelings. This planning was then turned into a storyboard using StoryTribe that features a user named Alexis who uses the adoption service to find a senior cat at a local shelter.

PROTOTYPE #3

Speculative design artefact.

The speculative design artefact is a preliminary mock-up of the homepage for the senior animal adoption service on mobile devices. Created using Figma, the screens show an expanded representation of what a user would see if they scrolled through the full homepage ‚Äì this includes any featured news, adoptable animals who have been listed the longest, local shelters, and helpful caretaking articles for novice and veteran pet owners alike.


The design of the interface draws inspiration from existing animal adoption services such as Petfinder and Adopt a Pet. Given my storyboard scenario features the user coming across a post about Senior Pet Awareness Month, I felt it would be important to include this somehow in the artefact, since many animals shelters and organizations tend to promote senior animal adoptions during this month and are able to direct adopter interest to this age demographic. The decision to feature ‚Äúlong-timers‚Äù on the homepage aims to help animals who have been listed the longest to find homes faster, as prior research shows that animal health and well-being decreases as the time they spend in a shelter increases.

The voice and language used within the interface for this artefact is playful, yet simple and clear. When researching existing animal adoption services, I noticed that Adopt a Pet made use of clever puns and phrases associated with pets (ex. displaying ‚ÄúSit! Stay! We're fetching our four-legged friends‚Äù as the page loads search results). I wanted to incorporate this sense of humour while ensuring that the copy was straightforward for users and wasn‚Äôt overly reliant on pet puns (ex. the ‚ÄúTabby Tidbits‚Äù section title, the descriptions of the featured help articles).
